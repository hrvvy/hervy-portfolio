Case study · Consumer / DTC · 2026

Every new customer was costing the company money.

DrinkAid sells anti-hangover supplements in Singapore. The founders ran their own ads to a product page where the S$17.90 single pouch was the default trial choice, against a S$40–50 cost of acquisition. The maths does not need much commentary: growth meant losing money faster.

Engagement: Business Elevates Visit the gummies lander ↗

The situation

The founders were doing what most DTC founders do under pressure: cycling through borrowed ecommerce tactics, each one fixing a symptom. New creative, new discounts, new landing page sections. Meanwhile the structural problem sat untouched: the cheapest possible entry point was also the most popular one, so most first orders arrived below water before the ad bill was even counted.

This is the classic disguise. Ad metrics get blamed for what is really an offer architecture problem, because the ad account is where the losses become visible.

The diagnosis

Before touching a single campaign, I audited the account and built a unit-economics simulator across the entire pricing ladder: COGS, shipping, payment fees, and the break-even ROAS required at every bundle size. The spreadsheet said what the ad account had been trying to say for months. At a S$17.90 default order, no realistic ad performance could produce profit; the offer required a 1.87× ROAS just to break even, on thin margin.

The simulator also showed the way out. At higher bundle sizes the margin structure inverted: better gross margin per order, a lower break-even bar, and enough contribution to pay for acquisition on the first order. The problem was never the ads. It was what the ads were pointed at.

Where the work concentrated

The full sequence, with this engagement's heaviest stages highlighted
Economics→ Market→ Customer→ Positioning→ Offer→ Landing page→ Creative→ Campaigns→ Measurement→ Systems

This engagement is the sequence in miniature: economics first, then the offer the economics demanded, then the pages and campaigns to carry it.

What we built

1

Modelled the economics before spending a dollar

The unit-economics simulator became the decision engine for everything after it: which bundle sizes to sell, what discounts the margin could survive, and what CAC the front end had to hit.

2

Rebuilt the front-end offer around bundles

The single-pouch trial gave way to 5, 7, and 9-pouch buy-N-get-free bundles. Same product, restructured economics: higher order values, 66.7% gross margin, and first orders that fund their own acquisition.

3

Created the Clear2X™ positioning

Instead of competing as another hangover remedy, the product got a proprietary mechanism: Clear2X™, built around increased ALDH2 enzyme expression. A reason it works that competitors cannot claim word for word.

4

Built a dedicated landing page and new campaigns

Traffic stopped landing on a generic product page and started landing on a page built to sell one decision: which bundle. New campaigns replaced the founder-run structure, and front-end CAC fell to S$15–20.
