Case study · Health & wellness · 2022–present

Lead costs were not the problem. Being a commodity was.

Biohackk was a S$50–70K/month weight-loss business paying S$250–500 per lead, hovering around break-even on acquisition. The founders assumed they had a media buying problem. What they had was a positioning problem wearing a media buying costume.

Engagement: growth consultant, then Business Elevates Visit biohackk.com ↗

The situation

In 2022, Biohackk sold a ~S$2.5K weight-loss programme into one of the most saturated markets in Singapore. Every competitor promised the same outcome in roughly the same words, so Meta priced everyone's leads like the commodities they were: S$250–500 each. Of the leads that did arrive, only 11% turned into appointments. Acquisition roughly broke even, which meant the business could stay alive but could not afford to grow.

The instinct in that position is to fix the ads: new creative, new audiences, new agency. The account had already been through that cycle. The numbers barely moved, which should have been the first clue.

The diagnosis

When a market cannot tell you apart from your competitors, the auction makes you pay for the resemblance. Undifferentiated positioning shows up in the metrics as expensive leads and weak conversion, which is why it gets misdiagnosed as a marketing problem. The real gap was commercial: no owned position in the market, an offer priced and packaged like everyone else's, and a customer journey that treated a S$2.5K decision like an impulse buy.

So the engagement did not start in Ads Manager. It started with the economics, the customer, and the question every repositioning has to answer: what can this brand say that competitors cannot copy by tomorrow morning?

Where the work concentrated

The full sequence, with this engagement's heaviest stages highlighted
Economics→ Market→ Customer→ Positioning→ Offer→ Landing page→ Creative→ Campaigns→ Measurement→ Systems

Nearly the whole sequence ran here, which is what a multi-year engagement looks like. The stages that changed the trajectory were positioning and offer; everything downstream inherited their economics.

What we built

1

Repositioned around a proprietary mechanism

Instead of promising weight loss harder than everyone else, the brand was rebuilt around effortless fat loss driven by a named, ownable mechanism: ToxiFat™. Naming the mechanism gave customers a new explanation for why past attempts failed, and gave the brand ground competitors could not stand on.

2

Re-engineered the offer and pricing architecture

The single ~S$2.5K programme became a ladder of programmes from S$5K to S$30K, matched to how much support each customer segment actually wanted. Premium service delivery from the founders and operations team is what made the higher tiers honest rather than aspirational.

3

Rebuilt the landing pages and creative strategy

Every asset was rewritten and redesigned to carry the new positioning, so a S$2.5K-to-S$30K decision met a brand that looked and read like it deserved the price.

4

Fixed the journey between lead and appointment

The handoff from ad to consultation was redesigned around the customer's actual decision process. Appointment rate went from 11% to 30%, which changed the economics of every dollar spent upstream.

5

Evolved the category itself

As the work compounded, the company outgrew weight loss entirely and moved to metabolic health: a category with a longer customer relationship and room the old market never had.

Three years on, the lead-cost reduction has held. Positioning did what media buying could not.
