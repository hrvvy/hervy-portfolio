Case study · Infant nutrition / FMCG · 2026

You cannot buy ads for a S$6 box. You can for a S$100 pantry.

HLTone makes baby snacks that sell for S$5–8 a box. At that price point, paid acquisition cannot pay for itself: even a modest cost per click eats the entire order. The brand did not need better marketing. It needed an order value that could afford marketing at all.

Engagement: Business Elevates Visit the pantry page ↗

The situation

FMCG brands feel this trap most sharply. The product is good, parents love it, retail velocity is fine, and yet every attempt at paid acquisition dies on the spreadsheet. When a S$5–8 box has to absorb customer acquisition, shipping, and payment fees, there is no creative brilliant enough to make the arithmetic work.

The usual responses are to discount harder or to abandon DTC for retail entirely. Both give up the most valuable thing a brand can own: a direct relationship with the household buying it.

The diagnosis

This was an AOV problem; the ad account was just where it sent the bill. No household buys one box of baby snacks and stops; they buy a stream of them for months. The catalogue just was not shaped like the customer's actual behaviour. The unit of purchase was a box, when the unit of consumption was a pantry.

Restructure the catalogue around how families actually consume, and order values move into territory where paid media becomes a lever instead of a leak.

Where the work concentrated

The full sequence, with this engagement's heaviest stages highlighted
Economics→ Market→ Customer→ Positioning→ Offer→ Landing page→ Creative→ Campaigns→ Measurement→ Systems

The work stayed deliberately upstream: until the offer architecture could support acquisition, building campaigns would have been spending money to prove the spreadsheet right.

What we built

1

Restructured single boxes into bundles

The first step moved the default purchase from one box to several, lifting the order floor without asking the customer to change behaviour; families were already buying multiples, just one transaction at a time.

2

Evolved bundles into pantry systems

Bundles became a catalogue of pantry systems, from discovery sets for first-time buyers to family snack pantries. The offer changed from selling a box to stocking a household, and order values followed: S$40 to S$108.

A pantry, not a box: the catalogue as it sells today.

3

Developed GentleMelt™ and StageWise™ positioning

Two proprietary frames differentiate the snacks in a crowded aisle: GentleMelt™ for texture safety, StageWise™ for developmental stage. They give parents a reason to choose beyond price, and give the brand claims competitors cannot copy word for word. StageWise™ now leads the brand's own homepage: "Singapore's first age-calibrated functional snacking system."

The numbers

Order valueS$5–8 per box→S$40–108

Engagement ongoing; the catalogue restructure is the foundation for the paid acquisition phase that follows it.

The product did not change. The unit of purchase did.
